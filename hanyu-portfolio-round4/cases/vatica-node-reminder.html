The mess

VATICA outsources garment production and tracks every order in one Excel sheet: 680 live orders × 37 schedule columns — costing sign-off, three-stage approval, fabric & trim purchase, pre-production samples, bulk production, delivery. Planners chased each node by memory and by shouting across departments. When I profiled a real snapshot: 304 orders short on fabric, 325 short on trims, and of 207 pre-production samples due, only 4 had a completion date filled in.

The insight

Strip away the Excel and SMTP shell, and the whole follow-up SOP is three expressions:

days    = (due − today).dt.days     # distance to "now"
warn    = days.between(0, AHEAD)    # inside look-ahead → fire
overdue = days < 0                  # crossed zero → escalate
It's a threshold trigger in a distance field. Every node is a dot; time is the wave; the look-ahead window is the wave's radius. Nothing needs AI — the risk logic is pure arithmetic on dates.

What shipped

A local Python pipeline that reads the tracking sheet every morning, applies the rule to all 680 orders, groups hits by owner, and sends one email per person through the company's existing NetEase mailbox — purchasing gets its fabric list, each sales rep gets their own orders, overdue rows pinned red on top. A config workbook (contacts, rules, thresholds) is the only thing staff ever touch. Reminders stop the moment someone backfills the completion date — which quietly fixed the data-hygiene problem too.

Why the simulation exists

To explain the system to a non-technical CEO — and to make the abstraction visible — I rebuilt the trigger as the live piece on the left. Drag the response rate down and watch red pile up left of the TODAY line; widen the look-ahead window and watch issues surface before they cascade. The argument makes itself.

What I'd tell another team

The most valuable move wasn't writing code — it was noticing that the alert logic never needed a model. Deadline math is deterministic; language models only earn their keep at the edges (drafting customer-facing emails). Separating those two concerns is what made the tool free to run, trivially auditable, and deployable inside a company that can't use foreign AI services at all.
