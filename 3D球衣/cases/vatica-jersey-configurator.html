VATICA Jersey Configurator — a fitting room for a jersey that doesn't exist yet

The mess

Vatica's own site has a page called "Design Your Own Teamwear" — MOQ of one piece, real customization on offer. But the page itself is a grid of flat, pre-rendered mockups: three basketball colorways, each shown once, front and back, with a placeholder team name ("ABCDE") and a placeholder number ("23") baked into the image. A buyer picking a fourth colorway, or wanting to see their own team's name on the shirt, has no way to preview it — they email a rep and wait for a proof.

The insight

I went looking for a better interaction pattern and landed on two references: recent.design, a design-inspiration gallery, and Bécane Paris' lookbook, which has a "spin the model, then click into a garment" flow that reads as remarkably premium. Opening it up, the "3D" isn't 3D at all — it's a photographed image sequence, a turntable of real photos scrubbed by degree. That's a legitimate, cheap trick, but it has a ceiling: every colorway needs its own photoshoot, and the name/number are baked in, exactly like Vatica's current page.

A bent plane with a canvas texture gets you most of the way to "real 3D" for a fraction of a garment simulation — and unlike a photo sequence, every colorway is free, because nothing was ever photographed in the first place.

What shipped

A basketball-jersey silhouette (V-neck front, crew-neck back) is drawn as a 2D outline, extruded for a sliver of real thickness, then bent around a vertical cylinder — front and back panels share one radius, so their side seams meet with no gap. Each panel gets its own live <canvas> texture: body color, a fading side-panel accent, collar/hem trim, and the team's number, redrawn the instant a color, name, or number changes. Three preset colorways echo Vatica's own catalog; a fourth opens free color pickers. Drag left/right to spin the jersey — the degree readout and FRONT/BACK snap buttons are a small homage to the Bécane viewer this started from.

What I'd tell another team

The hardest bug wasn't the 3D bend math — it was text. Wrapping a flat canvas around a cylinder and viewing the far side from "outside" introduces a mirror most people don't expect; I spent longer than I'd like to admit chasing a phantom "geometry mirror" before realizing the actual bug was a stray rotation in my own arc-text helper. Ground-truth before you theorize — a plain debug label in the corner of each texture would have shown the real problem in one screenshot instead of three rounds of trigonometry.

The bigger lesson is about honesty in a demo: this isn't a garment simulation, and I'm not presenting it as one. It's a cheap illusion — two curved planes and a repainted texture — built to answer one question fast: does seeing your own name and colors on the shirt, live, change how a buyer feels about ordering it? For that question, this is enough, and it costs nothing to keep offering new colorways once it exists.
